Spousal benefit: why Jane gets more

Jane's own SS benefit at her FRA (age 67, July 2035) would be $1,376/mo. She is also entitled to a spousal benefit = 50% of John's PIA (his FRA benefit).

John's PIA = $3,817/mo → 50% spousal = $1,909/mo
Jane's own benefit = $1,376/mo
Social Security pays the higher: $1,909/mo (spousal)

Key rules: (1) Spousal is based on John's PIA, not his claiming age. (2) If Jane claims before her FRA (67), the spousal benefit is permanently reduced — she must wait to 67 for the full 50%. (3) No delayed credits on spousal past FRA. (4) John must file first. (5) After John passes, Jane receives a survivor benefit = 100% of John's actual benefit — making John's claiming age consequential for the household for life.

Pension & the survivor lever

John's pension is $1,500/mo nominal with no COLA, starting at age 60. In real terms it shrinks every year — at 3% inflation it loses ~60% of purchasing power over 30 years. There is no survivor benefit; it stops at John's death. John's SS survivor lever: at age 70 ($4,733/mo) vs age 62 ($2,688/mo), the survivor premium is $2,045/mo (~$24,500/yr) for every remaining year of Jane's life.

How Real Annual ROI is calculated

Method: Incremental IRR vs. Age-62 baseline. For each claiming age (64–70), delaying is an investment: cost = foregone benefits during the wait; return = lifetime surplus of higher household SS (John's + Jane's spousal/survivor). We find the annualized real rate r making NPV of incremental cash flows = 0. Positive = waiting beats claiming at 62 in real terms.

IRA RMD methodology

John's IRA value at age 75 is set by the slider in future (nominal) dollars at age 75 (RMD start). To show in today's purchasing power, that nominal amount is discounted back using the inflation rate. The IRA annual growth rate slider is a nominal rate — the real (inflation-adjusted) growth applied to the real balance each year is (1 + nominal growth) ÷ (1 + inflation) − 1. At default settings (3% growth, 3% inflation) the real balance is essentially flat before RMDs, so the IRA depletes purely from withdrawals. Each year's RMD = prior year-end balance ÷ IRS Uniform Lifetime Table factor for the IRA owner's age that year. After John passes, Jane inherits the IRA; RMDs continue using the table at Jane's age (surviving spouse treating as own). All values shown in today's dollars.

Section 3 — Taxable Social Security Amount

How taxable Social Security is calculated (IRS Publication 915)

Provisional income (PI) = non-SS income (pension + wage + taxable interest + IRA RMD) + 50% of combined household SS benefits. Taxable SS is computed via the IRS two-tier formula:

Married Filing Jointly:
Exempt PI < $32,000 → $0 taxable SS
Tier 1 $32,000 – $44,000 → lesser of 50%×(PI−$32k) or 50%×SS
Tier 2 PI > $44,000 → lesser of [$6,000 + 85%×(PI−$44k)] or 85%×SS

Single (after first spouse passes):
Exempt PI < $25,000 → $0 taxable SS
Tier 1 $25,000 – $34,000 → lesser of 50%×(PI−$25k) or 50%×SS
Tier 2 PI > $34,000 → lesser of [$4,500 + 85%×(PI−$34k)] or 85%×SS

Maximum taxable SS is always capped at 85% of combined household SS benefits.
⚠️ Frozen thresholds — getting worse every year: These bracket limits were set in the 1980s–1990s and have never been COLA-adjusted. As Social Security benefits and other income grow with inflation over time, more and more of each retiree's SS benefit crosses these fixed thresholds and becomes taxable. What once exempted most retirees now catches the vast majority — and the real value of the exempt threshold shrinks further every year.
⚡ The Social Security Torpedo: Every extra dollar of non-SS income can push up to $1.85 of income into taxation — the extra dollar itself plus up to $0.85 more SS becoming taxable. This creates an effective marginal rate well above the stated bracket until SS is fully taxed (85% maximum).

Section 4 — Total Income Tax

How total tax is calculated

AGI = all non-SS income + taxable SS amount. Taxable income = AGI − standard deduction ($32,000 MFJ / $16,000 single in 2026 dollars). Tax brackets applied at filing status for that year (MFJ while both alive, Single after first death).
